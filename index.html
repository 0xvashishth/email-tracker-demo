Approaches

4 Tracking Methods

Industry standard approaches used by email platforms today.

Most common

🖼 Tracking Pixel

1×1 transparent GIF/PNG with a unique URL per email. Server logs every fetch request. Used by Titan, Mailchimp, HubSpot, and virtually every ESP.

✓ Silent, no user action needed ✓ Works in HTML emails ✗ Blocked by Apple Mail Privacy ✗ Image blocking defeats it
Most reliable

🔗 Link Tracking

Every link is redirected through a tracking URL. A click definitively confirms the email was read. Used alongside pixel tracking.

✓ Confirms real engagement ✓ Not blocked by privacy tools ✗ Only fires on actual clicks ✗ URL rewriting looks suspicious
Opt-in only

📨 Read Receipts (MDN)

Standard email protocol (Message Disposition Notification). Asks the client to send a read confirmation. Requires recipient approval.

✓ Explicit consent built in ✓ Protocol-standard ✗ Disabled by default in most clients ✗ Recipient sees the request
Advanced / fragile

🎨 CSS / Font Requests

Loading external CSS files or web fonts triggers HTTP requests. Less reliable — most clients strip external CSS or cache aggressively.

✓ Harder to block than pixels ✗ Stripped by Gmail, Outlook ✗ Inconsistent across clients ✗ Not worth the complexity
Gotchas

Known Limitations

Every major email platform deals with these.

🍎

Apple Mail Privacy (iOS 15+)

Apple pre-fetches all email images in the background. Every email to an Apple Mail user shows as "opened" — even unread ones. Affects ~50% of mobile email.

🏢

Corporate image blocking

Outlook and many enterprise clients block images by default. Real opens are missed unless the user clicks "show images". Common in B2B email.

🔁

Google pre-fetching

Gmail's proxy sometimes fetches images for spam scanning before the user opens. Can show false opens with a Google IP rather than the real recipient's location.

📦

Email caching

Some clients cache images after first load. Re-opens won't fire a second pixel request. Open count may be understated for repeated reads.

🌐

VPN / proxy

The IP you log belongs to the proxy or VPN the recipient uses — not their real location. Geolocation from open events is unreliable.

⚖️

GDPR / Privacy law

EU (GDPR), Canada (CASL) require disclosing tracking in your privacy policy. Always honor unsubscribes. Tracking without disclosure can be a violation.

Deliverability

3 DNS Records Every Sender Needs

Without these, Gmail and Yahoo will reject or spam-folder your email — tracked or not.

SPF

Sender Policy Framework

A DNS TXT record that lists which servers are authorized to send email for your domain. Prevents spoofing. Without it: Gmail may reject your mail outright.

v=spf1 include:_spf.yourdomain.com ~all
DKIM

DomainKeys Identified Mail

Adds a cryptographic signature to outgoing emails. Proves the email wasn't altered in transit. Without it: marked as suspicious by most clients.

yourdomain._domainkey → TXT → p=MIGfMA0...
DMARC

Domain-based Message Auth

Policy that tells receiving servers what to do when SPF/DKIM fail: quarantine or reject. Greatly improves deliverability scores. Required by Gmail for bulk senders since 2024.

v=DMARC1; p=quarantine; rua=mailto:...

Why Localhost Fails

Gmail vs Titan: The Key Difference

This is exactly why your localhost pixel returned 404 — and how Titan avoids it.

❌ Your localhost setup

http://localhost:3000/pixel/abc123
  • Only reachable on your machine
  • Gmail proxy can't reach it
  • ngrok free tier gets throttled
  • URL resets on every restart
  • Google rejects non-standard domains

✅ Titan's production setup

https://track.titan.email/pixel/abc123
  • Permanent, public HTTPS domain
  • Gmail proxy can reach it 24/7
  • Trusted domain, no blocks
  • Always-on infrastructure
  • Valid TLS certificate

🔍 How Gmail's Image Proxy works

Since 2013, Gmail routes all images through Google's own proxy servers (ci3.googleusercontent.com). When a recipient opens your email, Google's servers fetch the pixel — not the recipient's device. This means your pixel server must be publicly reachable from the internet with a valid HTTPS URL. This is also why Gmail sometimes shows "opened" before the recipient even looks — Google pre-fetches images for spam scanning.
